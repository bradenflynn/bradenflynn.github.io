Case Study: NVIDIA Analysis

NVIDIA Business Case & Valuation

Using Python to Simluate Big Business Decisions

01. The Goal

NVIDIA is one of the most talked-about companies in the world. I wanted to build a model that looks past the hype and uses real financial math to see if two things make sense:

  1. Hiring: Does it actually pay off to hire 2,000 new engineers right now?
  2. Stock Price: Is NVIDIA really worth $190 per share, or is that just market excitement?

02. How I Got the Numbers

1. Calculating the "Hurdle Rate" (WACC)

To know if an investment is good, you first have to know how much profit the company needs to make just to pay its bills and keep investors happy. I combined the interest rates on government bonds with a "risk score" for NVIDIA's stock. I found that NVIDIA needs to make at least 11.3% profit on any new project for it to be worth doing.

2. The "Time Machine" (DCF)

I built a "Discounted Cash Flow" (DCF) model. Think of this as a time machine for money. I predicted how much cash NVIDIA will likely generate over the next 10 years, and then calculated what that future cash is worth in today's dollars. This revealed a fair stock price of about $108.

3. The 3-Year "Learning Curve"

Engineers aren't 100% productive on their first day. My model simulates a "ramp-up" period where the new hires gradually contribute more revenue over 3 years. Even with this conservative start, the hiring plan still earns a 37% return, which is way above our 11.3% hurdle rate from Step 1.

03. Final Verdict

The Business Move: YES

Hiring the 2,000 engineers is a massive win. For every dollar spent on them, the company generates significantly more in value. It's an efficient use of capital.

The Stock Price: CAUTION

At $190, the stock is "expensive." It assumes NVIDIA will grow at 32% every single year for the next decade. While possible, it leaves very little room for error.
